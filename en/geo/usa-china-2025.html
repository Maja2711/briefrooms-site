USA–China: Scenarios for 2025

A 3-minute briefing: five plausible paths, key drivers, and early-warning indicators.

Why this matters

US–China competition sets the tone for global trade, technology, and security. 2025 is close enough for concrete planning and far enough for surprises. Below are five compact scenarios you can compare at a glance.

1) Managed Competition (baseline, ~35%)

Rivalry continues but crisis channels remain open; export controls are stable; selective tech carve-outs allow limited cooperation (climate, pandemic tools, aviation safety).

  • Drivers: election incentives for stability; business lobby; allies prefer predictability.
  • Signals: regular defense hotlines; narrow but real visas/academic reopenings.

2) Sanctions Spiral (~20%)

A new flashpoint (semiconductors or rare earths) triggers tit-for-tat sanctions; supply chains reroute fast; global growth dips 0.3–0.5 pp.

  • Drivers: export-control breaches; domestic pressure after an incident.
  • Signals: expanded entity lists; tighter outbound-investment rules; shipping insurance spikes.

3) Quiet Thaw (~18%)

Practical deals in fentanyl precursors, aviation, and financial supervision; tariffs unchanged but enforcement softens; visas rise.

  • Drivers: growth headwinds in both economies; European mediation.
  • Signals: joint statements after ministerial meetings; mid-tier tech MOUs.

4) Taiwan Shock (~15%)

Non-kinetic blockade tests (customs/air-defense zones) force emergency G7 coordination; freight and insurance jump; chips face weeks-long delays.

  • Drivers: local political missteps; PLA exercises near critical lanes.
  • Signals: long NOTAMs; “inspection” regimes; large live-fire drills overlapping shipping routes.

5) Tech Cold Peace (~12%)

Both sides formalize a “red box” of restricted tech (AI accelerators, EDA, advanced lithography) but open “green lanes” for clean energy and health.

  • Drivers: corporate pressure + climate targets.
  • Signals: joint standards groups; track-2 AI safety workshops with officials observing.

What to watch (practical)

  • Policy: US outbound-investment screening scope; China’s export-permit cadence for gallium/graphite.
  • Markets: freight to/from East Asia, marine insurance premia, semiconductor lead times.
  • Security: frequency and duration of PLA drills; hotline usage after close encounters.
